## Findings

### 1 — NOAA Weather Radio (162.549 MHz)
Nearest standard channel: **WX7 at 162.550 MHz** (within 1 kHz of nominal — within
RTL-SDR frequency accuracy). SNR of 23.2 dB and a standard deviation of only 0.21 dBm
confirm a continuous, unmodulated FM broadcast carrier. The signal never dropped during
the scan. The waterfall shows a solid, unwavering bright band at this frequency.

### 2 — Land-mobile / government repeater (172.031 MHz)
The strongest signal detected (SNR 24.3 dB, mean 15.0 dBm). 172 MHz falls in the US
**170–174 MHz government land-mobile allocation** used by federal agencies and public
safety. The stability profile (std: 0.22 dBm) is identical to the NOAA transmitter,
indicating a continuously keyed carrier — almost certainly a squelch-tail repeater or
a sub-audible CTCSS-coded system. The high power (15 dBm mean) suggests the transmitter
is geographically close.

### 3 — Marine VHF working traffic (157.748 MHz)
The standout anomaly in the dataset. The standard deviation of **6.24 dBm** is 30×
higher than the STRONG signals, and the mean-to-peak spread is 14 dBm (mean −6.3,
peak +7.7). This is the signature of an intermittent transmitter — on during bursts,
silent otherwise. Three distinct burst events are visible in the waterfall (12:10:30,
12:11:00, 12:11:10). 157.748 MHz corresponds to a Marine VHF working channel; the
pattern is consistent with a vessel or shore station transmitting data or voice calls.

### 4–7 — Marginal carriers (168–173 MHz)
All five marginal signals fall in the 167–173 MHz land-mobile band. The four stable
ones (std < 0.55 dBm) are real distant carriers at the sensitivity threshold — likely
government or public-utility repeaters beyond practical voice range. Signal 7
(173.317 MHz, std: 2.18 dBm) shows variable behaviour, suggesting either a pulsed
beacon or sporadic traffic.
